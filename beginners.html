Getting Started
Beginners have several options! The easiest path is to check the calendar for the next class or guided practica. Most of our events are drop-in, meaning you can show up without advance registration for either members or non-members. A beginner-friendly class or guided practica is the ideal first step.
No — many people come alone and that's completely normal. Classes typically rotate partners so that leaders and followers both get the experience of dancing with different people. This helps you develop flexibility and adaptability rather than depending on one person's style.
Wear casual, comfortable, breathable clothing you can move freely in. For shoes, the key is being able to slide your foot along the floor and pivot easily. Flat leather-soled shoes or smooth-soled dress shoes work well. Avoid rubber-soled shoes (they grip and can torque your knees) and steel-toe boots. Women may eventually wear tango heels, but comfortable flats are recommended when starting out.

About Argentine Tango
Argentine tango is an improvisational partner dance — it's about being in the moment, listening to your partner and the music, and creating movement together without a fixed routine. Ballroom tango is choreographed and performed to a set sequence. Argentine tango emphasizes technique, connection, and communication between partners, which is why it never gets old — every dance is unique.
The word "milonga" has two meanings in tango:
  1. A social dance event — a party where people dance tango without instruction. Think of it as the tango equivalent of a dance. Dressing up is expected and encouraged.
  2. A music style — milonga music is in 2/4 time and has an energetic, rhythmic feel that's faster than a standard tango.
A practica is an open practice session where dancers work on technique, try new movements, and ask questions — unlike a milonga where the social rules are stricter. A guided practica has an instructor available to help. Both types use tango, milonga, and vals music played in sets, and dancers practice with different partners throughout the session.
Tanda: A set of three or four songs by the same orchestra or performer, in the same style (tango, milonga, or vals). You typically dance with the same partner for an entire tanda.

Cortina: The word means "curtain" in Spanish. It's a short musical interlude — usually 30–40 seconds of non-tango music — that signals the end of a tanda. It's the cue to thank your partner and find someone new for the next set.
Cabeceo (cab-eh-SAY-oh) is the traditional Argentine tango invitation to dance: a lead makes eye contact from across the room and gives a slight nod of the head. If the follower is interested, they return eye contact and a nod. This system allows for discreet, rejection-free invitations — if you don't make eye contact, no one is embarrassed. It's used at milongas and is good practice at practicas as well.
Line of dance (LOD) means that couples move in a counter-clockwise rotation around the outer edge of the dance floor, like cars on a one-way track. This keeps the floor organized and safe. Good floor craft means: following LOD, not stepping backwards into the lane behind you, and not cutting across other couples. On a crowded floor, quick mutual apologies suffice for inevitable small collisions.
Etiquette
Be friendly, patient, and supportive. If something isn't working, phrase it constructively — "Can we try that again?" rather than placing blame. Remember: everyone is still learning, no matter how long they've been dancing. Positive feedback and a good attitude make practice better for everyone.
Dress your tango best! If the milonga is at a private home venue, bring refreshments to share. Do not give instruction or corrections on the dance floor — save feedback for practicas. Dance what you know confidently. Use cabeceo to invite partners. Be a considerate floorcraft partner and respect the line of dance.
